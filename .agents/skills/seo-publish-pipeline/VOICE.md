# Voice: Evidence-Led Urgency

Use urgency to help the reader act on a real problem now. Borrow the mechanics,
never wording, from strong direct-response SEO copy.

## Core pattern

Where the evidence supports it, use this sequence:

1. **Name the costly status quo** - state what the reader is losing, delaying,
   or making harder. Make the consequence specific to the topic.
2. **Show the better outcome** - describe the useful result in plain language,
   not an abstract promise.
3. **Make the next move concrete** - tell the reader what to check, fix, compare,
   or try first.
4. **Add proof nearby** - use a verified number, product fact, example, or
   source. If there is no proof, lower the certainty or remove the claim.

Prefer short, decisive constructions: problem then fix, traffic then customers,
guessing then evidence, long list then first priority. A compact contrast can
make the choice clear, but do not force every section into the same template.

### Replacement proof

The strongest transformation often shows what the customer no longer needed,
followed by the better result they achieved. Use a tight before/after shape:

- Before: the costly workaround, recurring spend, manual task, or uncertainty.
- After: the verified outcome, preferably in the customer's own plain words.

This is stronger than saying the product is "powerful" because the tradeoff is
visible. Use it in testimonials, case-study transitions, and product sections
only when both halves are documented. Preserve the customer's meaning and
level of certainty. Never turn correlation into causation or strengthen a
modest result during editing.

## Where to turn it up

- **Title and meta:** lead with the useful outcome or concrete consequence when
  it fits the keyword naturally. Keep the exact keyword front-loaded.
- **Section headings:** name the reader's problem or desired result, not the
  category of information. Prefer "Fix pages Google can't index" over
  "Technical SEO considerations."
- **Body transitions:** after explaining a risk, state the first practical move.
- **Product section:** connect a verified capability to time saved, risk reduced,
  or a decision made. Trace both the capability and outcome to `product.md`.
- **CTA:** use one imperative action tied to the article's intent and immediate
  payoff. Make clear what happens after the click.

Keep the short answer, longer answer block, definitions, instructions, and FAQ
primarily informational. Clarity outranks urgency there.

## Credibility guardrails

- Never invent scarcity, deadlines, availability, prices, savings, losses,
  competitor behavior, or consequences.
- Never invent or composite a testimonial. Quote only documented customer
  wording with its real attribution and source; otherwise paraphrase accurately
  and label it as a paraphrase.
- Never imply that delay causes damage unless the article provides evidence or
  makes the mechanism explicit.
- Do not use fear without a practical next step.
- Do not shame the reader or attack a named competitor.
- Do not copy memorable phrases, headings, sentence sequences, or testimonials
  from reference sites. Recreate the principle in the project's own voice.
- Use at most one urgency or contrast beat per section. Repetition turns pressure
  into noise.
- If calm wording is more accurate for the intent, use calm wording.

Exit check: highlight every urgent sentence. For each one, identify the real
consequence, the supporting evidence, and the next action. Rewrite or cut any
sentence missing one of those three.
